     Gregg Shorthand is Easy.  The writing system and its principles are easy to learn. Since it is based on the same strokes that are used to form cursive letters, it is very natural and certainly easier to write than longhand is.
     Many of the letters you have learned in longhand have several different forms (capital, lowercase, cursive, print, calligraphic, etc.).  In Gregg Shorthand, each sound is represented by only one stroke.  Practice regularly and your skill in shorthand will improve rapidly.

Principles

Group A

     1. Silent Letters Omitted.  In the English language, many words contain letters that are not pronounced.  In Gregg Shorthand we omit all silent letters and write only those sounds that we hear in a word.  For example, for the word say we writes s-a, omitting the y, which is not pronounced; for the word knee we write n-e, as the k and the final e are not pronounced; for the word cat we write k-a-t, as the c is pronounced k.

     2. S-Z.  The first stroke you will learn is the shorthand s, which is one of the most common letters in the English language.  It is approximately the same size as the longhand comma and is written downward.  Because the s and z are often quite intelligible with each other, the same stroke is used for both, as in the words saves and seize.

     4. F, V.  The stroke for f is the same as that of the s, but it is larger—about half the height between the lines in Gregg Ruled paper.  V is the same shape, but it is even larger—the entire height of the space between the lines of the notebook.  These two strokes are written downward.

     Did you notice that the final s in saves has the z sound?

     5. E.  The stroke for e is a tiny circle.  It is simple the longhand e with the two connecting strokes omitted.

     Did you notice that the y in easy is pronounced e and therefore is represented by the e circle?

Group B

     6. N, M.  The stroke for n is a very short straight line; the shorthand stroke for m is a longer straight line.  Both n and m are written forward.  It is from this system of writing that the printing terms en dash (–) and em dash (—) derive.

knee [ne] mean [men] same [sam]

may [ma] seem [sem] vain [van]
main [man] sane [san] aim [am]

Group C

     8. O, R, L.  The shorthand stroke for o is a small and deep hook.  R is a short forward curve.  L is an even longer forward curve.

O [o] R [r] L [l]

know [no] so [so] own [on]
snow [sno] foe [fo] tone [ton]
toe [to] vote [vot] dome [dom]
dough [do] note [not] stone [ston]

     9. H.  The shorthand character for h is a got written about the following vowel. For speed purposes, the h dot may be omitted in words like had, has, her, and him.

     10. Omission of Minor Vowels.  Some vowels are so obscure or neutral that they are omitted when they do not contribute to speed or legibility.  For example, the e in the words taken and maker is absolutely useless, and is omitted.  Any vowel which does not contruble to the legibility of an outline may be omitted if its omission gives an outline that is easier to write.

     11. Punctuation and Capitalization.  In shorthand the following marks are used:

Period [.] Paragraph [*p] Parenthese [()]
Interrogation [?] Dash [--] Hyphen [-]

     For all other punctuation marks, the regular longhand forms are used.
     Capitalization is indicated by two short upward dashes underneath the word to be capitalized.
